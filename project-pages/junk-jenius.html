About The Assignment

The Junk Jenius: Plastic Bottle Recycling Machine is a project designed to make recycling easy, accessible, and fun. It is made for public spaces, like campuses, parks, and corporate offices. By automating the cutting, draining, and crushing of plastic bottles in a single streamlined process, it is able to reduce common barriers to recycling, like leftover liquids and the hassle of manual preparation. In short, it aims to optimize waste management by reducing volume and increasing recycling rates.
My contributions were the CAD design and the majority of the prototype build, with a focus on reclaiming scrap materials for sustainability. I utilized woodshop tools and manual machining practices to create a functional prototype. Additionally, I designed the final version to be playful and engaging, incorporating Pittsburgh-specific inspired aesthetics to connect with users. During the CMU Engineering Expo, our project won "Best Prototype," highlighting the functionality, creativity, and appeal of our design.

Project Goals

  • Simplify the disposal process for the user
  • Reduce waste volume to optimize transportation and disposal
  • Motivate users to recycle by improving engagement

Ideation Phase

Our brainstorming sessions focused on identifying barriers to recycling and designing a machine that addresses these issues. Initial sketches explored automation, hygiene, and durability. Key ideas included integrating a liquid drainage system and ensuring a one-button operation for ease of use.

Prototyping

We developed several prototypes, refining the design with each iteration. The final prototype includes a PVC chute for bottle insertion, a linear actuator for cutting and crushing, and a slanted ramp for liquid drainage. The process involved CAD modeling, 3D printing, and assembly using machine shop tools. The Pittsburgh-inspired design, featuring bridge-like structural elements, added a playful and local identity to the project, enhancing user engagement.

Final Result

The final design successfully integrates cutting, draining, and crushing into a single compact machine. It was well-received during user testing, with feedback highlighting its convenience and potential to enhance recycling rates in public spaces. The Pittsburgh-inspired aesthetic design and interactive functionality helped make the machine not just practical but also enjoyable to use.

Reflection

Through this project, I gained valuable experience in CAD design, prototyping, and team collaboration. The most rewarding aspect was seeing our concept evolve into a functioning prototype that addresses a real-world problem. Winning "Best Prototype" at the Engineering Expo was a proud moment, validating our hard work and creativity. Future iterations will focus on optimizing durability and exploring renewable energy integration.
