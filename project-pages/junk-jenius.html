Junk Jenius

October - December 2024

The Junk Jenius: Plastic Bottle Recycling Machine is a project designed to make recycling easy, accessible, and fun in public spaces (i.e. on our college campus). By automating the cutting, draining, and crushing of plastic bottles in a single streamlined process, it is able to reduce common barriers to recycling, like leftover liquids and the hassle of manual preparation.
My contributions were the CAD design and the majority of the prototype build, with a focus on reclaiming scrap materials for sustainability and making our machine fun and engaging. I built the prototype using primarily woodshop and manual machining practices. I also designed a final mockup to include Pittsburgh-inspired iconography to connect with our local users. During the Carnegie Mellon Engineering Expo, our project won "Best Prototype," validating our project's functionality, creativity, and mass appeal.

Project Goals

Objective: Address the inefficiencies in recycling plastic water bottles by creating an automated solution to compress bottles, reducing their volume, while draining residual liquids.

  • 1. Simplify the disposal process for the user
  • 2. Reduce waste volume to optimize transportation and disposal
  • 3. Motivate users to recycle by improving engagement

Research

Our brainstorming sessions focused on identifying barriers to recycling and designing a machine that addresses these issues. Initial sketches explored automation, hygiene, and durability. Key ideas included integrating a liquid drainage system and ensuring a one-button operation for ease of use.

Main Mechanism Prototyping

The first thing my team tackled was the most critical component: the crushing mechanism. Our initial approach used a linkage system that would convert a motor or hand crank's rotational motion into the linear motion, to crush the bottle. However, after sketching and drawing it out, it used more space than we would like.


So, we simplified the design to use an old off-the-shelf linear actuator borrowed from the school. This linear actuator was much more compact, with an appropriate range of motion for crushing a single bottle. The linear actuator would allow the user to easily control its motion via a 3-way switch. We tested the actuator with water bottles and very rudimentary mounting, and decided this solution would work!

Final Result

We developed several prototypes, refining the design with each iteration. The final prototype includes a PVC chute for bottle insertion, a linear actuator for cutting and crushing, and a slanted ramp for liquid drainage. The process involved CAD modeling, 3D printing, and assembly using machine shop tools. The Pittsburgh-inspired design, featuring bridge-like structural elements, added a playful and local identity to the project, enhancing user engagement. The final design successfully integrates cutting, draining, and crushing into a single compact machine. It was well-received during user testing, with feedback highlighting its convenience and potential to enhance recycling rates in public spaces. The Pittsburgh-inspired aesthetic design and interactive functionality helped make the machine not just practical but also enjoyable to use.

Reflection

Through this project, I gained valuable experience in CAD design, prototyping, and team collaboration. The most rewarding aspect was seeing our concept evolve into a functioning prototype that addresses a real-world problem. Winning "Best Prototype" at the Engineering Expo was a proud moment, validating our hard work and creativity. Future iterations will focus on optimizing durability and exploring renewable energy integration.
